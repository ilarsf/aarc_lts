# Coaching Language Guide

<div class="info-box tip">
  <h3>Effective Communication</h3>
  <p>How you communicate technical concepts can be as important as what you communicate. This guide provides effective terminology, metaphors, and communication strategies to help new scullers understand and apply proper technique.</p>
</div>

## Principles of Effective Coaching Communication

* **Be concise** - Use short, clear phrases that can be processed quickly
* **Be consistent** - Use the same terminology for the same concepts
* **Be specific** - Target exact body parts and movements
* **Be positive** - Frame instructions as what TO do rather than what NOT to do
* **Be visual** - Use metaphors and imagery that create clear mental pictures
* **Be patient** - Allow time for processing and questions

## Recommended Terminology Framework

### General Terminology Consistency

| Technical Term | Plain Language Alternative | Why It's Effective |
|----------------|---------------------------|-------------------|
| "Catch" | "Beginning of the stroke" | More intuitive for beginners |
| "Release/Finish" | "End of the stroke" | Clearly describes position |
| "Recovery" | "Return to the catch" | Describes purpose of movement |
| "Drive" | "Push phase" | Emphasizes leg action |
| "Feathering" | "Rotating the blade flat" | Describes visual action |
| "Squaring" | "Rotating the blade vertical" | Describes visual action |
| "Port/Starboard" | "Right/Left when facing forward" | Initially more intuitive |
| "Rating/SPM" | "Stroke speed" | More familiar concept |

### Position-Specific Terminology

| Rowing Term | Beginner-Friendly Description | When to Introduce |
|-------------|-------------------------------|-------------------|
| "Quarter slide" | "Short slide, knees just bent" | Session 1 |
| "Half slide" | "Medium slide position" | Session 1 |
| "Full slide" | "Complete compression" | Session 1-2 |
| "Arms away" | "Extend arms forward" | Session 1 |
| "Body over" | "Lean forward from the hips" | Session 1 |
| "Blades buried" | "Blades submerged in water" | Session 1 |
| "Extraction" | "Removing blades from water" | Session 2 |
| "Layback" | "Lean back slightly" | Session 2-3 |
| "Outside hand" | "Hand at end of oar" | Session 1 |
| "Inside hand" | "Hand closer to blade" | Later reference only |
| "Back splash" | "Water splash toward stern" | Session 3 |

## Effective Instructional Phrases

### Concise Coaching Cues

| Focus Area | Ineffective Phrase | Effective Phrase | Why It's Better |
|------------|-------------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| **Catch timing** | "Make sure your blades are squared before placing them in the water at the catch position" | "Square - Reach - Place" | Brief, sequential, actionable |
| **Drive initiation** | "You need to push with your legs first before you swing your back or pull with your arms" | "Legs first, then back, then arms" | Clear sequence, easy to remember |
| **Handle heights** | "Your hands are too low through the recovery which is causing your blades to sky above the water" | "Level hands, level blades" | Simple correlation between action and effect |
| **Recovery rate** | "You're rushing forward too quickly after the finish which is affecting your boat balance" | "Slow hands away, patient slide" | Actionable instruction with timing element |
| **Blade depth** | "Your blades are diving too deep into the water which creates drag and slows the boat" | "Light hands, just cover the blade" | Sensory cue with visual guidance |

### Building Instruction Sequences

For complex movements, build instructions in layers:
1. **First layer**: Single focus - "Push with your legs"
2. **Second layer**: Add next element - "Push with legs, then swing back"
3. **Final layer**: Complete sequence - "Push, swing, pull"

## Effective Metaphors and Imagery

### Body Position and Movement Metaphors

| Technical Concept | Effective Metaphor | Why It Works |
|-------------------|-------------------|-------------|
| **Posture** | "Sit like you're in a business meeting" | Creates image of tall, dignified posture |
| **Relaxed grip** | "Hold the oar like a bird: firm enough it won't fly away, gentle enough you won't hurt it" | Perfect tension reference |
| **Sequential power** | "Think of cracking a whip - energy transfers from handle to tip" | Illustrates power transfer through body |
| **Suspension** | "Hang your weight from the oar handle like hanging from a pull-up bar" | Creates feeling of connection and weight transfer |
| **Blade placement** | "Think of placing your blade on a shelf, not dropping it through the floor" | Horizontal vs. vertical movement |
| **Recovery balance** | "Move like you're walking on a tightrope" | Emphasizes control and balance |
| **Hand movement** | "Draw a straight line with your hands" | Visual path reference |

### Rhythm and Timing Metaphors

| Timing Element | Effective Metaphor | Application |
|----------------|-------------------|-------------|
| **Stroke ratio** | "Quick like a heartbeat, slow like a breath" | Contrasts quick drive with slow recovery |
| **Catch timing** | "Like touching a hot stove - quick contact" | Creates image of precise, quick movement |
| **Recovery sequence** | "Hands like honey, body like molasses, slide like syrup" | Progressively slower elements |
| **Drive emphasis** | "Explode like a spring, don't push like an elevator" | Emphasizes quick force application |
| **Overall rhythm** | "Rock - Paper - Scissors - Shoot" | Word pattern matching stroke rhythm |

## Teaching Different Learning Styles

### Visual Learners
* Use hand gestures to show movement paths
* Demonstrate techniques yourself when possible
* Reference observable landmarks ("blade should be at water level")
* Use diagrams or photos when available

### Auditory Learners
* Use rhythmic words to match stroke timing ("push-swing-pull")
* Describe sounds of good technique ("Listen for a clean catch")
* Ask them to verbalize the sequence back to you
* Use distinctive tones for different parts of stroke

### Kinesthetic Learners
* Guide hands-on adjustments (with permission)
* Focus on how movements should feel
* Use sensation-based cues ("feel the water pressure on the blade")
* Have them practice movements stationary first to build muscle memory

## Communicating Corrections

### The Correction Sandwich Method

1. **Positive element**: "Your leg drive is looking strong..."
2. **Correction**: "...now let's focus on keeping your hands level through the recovery..."
3. **Expected outcome**: "...which will help improve your blade height and make the catch cleaner."

### Problem-Solution Format

1. **Identify issue**: "Your blades are diving deep at the catch."
2. **Explain cause**: "This happens when hands drop too quickly."
3. **Provide solution**: "Try thinking about placing the blade on the surface, not pushing down."
4. **Reinforce with image**: "Like placing a glass on a table, not pushing it through."

## On-Water Communication Strategies

### Distance Communication

| Distance | Effective Approach | Example |
|----------|-------------------|---------|
| **Near (coaching launch alongside)** | Normal conversational tone, specific feedback | "Sarah, your hands are dropping at the finish." |
| **Medium (20-30ft)** | Projected voice, simplified cues | "HANDS UP AT THE FINISH!" |
| **Far (greater distance)** | Visual signals + megaphone, one-word cues | [Hands gesture up] "HANDS!" |

### Group vs. Individual Instructions

| Situation | Approach | Example |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| **Group-wide issue** | Address all athletes, use "we" language | "Let's all focus on our timing going into the catch." |
| **Individual correction in group** | Name individual, make correction, then generalize lesson | "Mark, your hands are dropping... Everyone remember to keep hands level." |
| **Individual major issue** | Signal to come closer or pull aside | "Emma, let's pause here. I'd like to work on your sequencing." |

## Special Communication Considerations

### For Anxious Participants
* Use calming, reassuring tones
* Break skills into smaller chunks
* Emphasize what's going well
* Acknowledge feelings: "It's normal to feel unstable at first"
* Give control: "Let's practice just the catch timing until you feel comfortable"

### For Physically Challenged Participants
* Focus on adaptations, not limitations
* Be direct about modifications: "Let's adjust your hand position to work with your range of motion"
* Emphasize universal principles that apply regardless of physical differences
* Ask questions: "How does that feel for your shoulder?"
* Invite participation: "What adjustments might help you perform this motion more comfortably?"

### For Technically Minded Participants
* Offer biomechanical explanations when appropriate
* Connect technique to physics principles: "This position creates optimal lever advantage"
* Use precise measurements and angles when relevant
* Acknowledge their analytical approach: "I can see you're thinking through this carefully"

## Creating a Feedback Culture

* **Encourage questions** - "What questions do you have about the recovery sequence?"
* **Check for understanding** - "Can you describe back to me the sequence we just practiced?"
* **Invite self-assessment** - "What do you feel is working well in your stroke right now?"
* **Normalize learning process** - "Every rower continually refines these skills"
* **Celebrate incremental progress** - "I noticed your catch timing improved significantly today"

<div class="info-box note">
  <h3>Cultural Sensitivity</h3>
  <p>Be mindful that metaphors and examples may have different meanings across cultural backgrounds. If you sense confusion, try alternative explanations and be open to adapting your language to be most effective for each participant.</p>
</div>
